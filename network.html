Networking and Building Connections

What Networking Is?

Networking can feel intimidating, especially if you associate it with “selling yourself.” In reality, networking is simply a way to learn from others and build professional relationships over time.

Networking is...

  • Learning from others by asking thoughtful questions about roles, teams, and career paths.
  • Building relationships over time through small, consistent interactions and follow-ups.
  • Being specific and respectful about what you are asking for (time, advice, or resources).
  • Offering value when you can, such as sharing a relevant article, thanking someone, or staying connected.
  • Practicing curiosity—focusing on understanding someone’s experience rather than impressing them.

Networking isn’t...

  • Asking for a job in the first message without building any context or connection.
  • Sending the same message to many people without personalizing it to their background or role.
  • Trying to “pitch” yourself with long paragraphs or excessive details.
  • Expecting immediate replies—many people are busy, and follow-up is normal.
  • Only reaching out when you need something, rather than maintaining relationships over time.

Tip: If you’re not sure what to say, start with a simple goal: learn one new thing. A short message and a clear question is often enough to begin a connection.

A Simple Networking Pathway

Networking does not need to be overwhelming. Following a simple, repeatable process can help you take action with confidence and reduce uncertainty at each step.

  1. Prepare
    Identify your goal, such as learning about a role or industry, and prepare a short introduction about who you are. Think about one or two questions you genuinely want to ask before reaching out.
  2. Reach Out
    Send a brief and personalized message that explains why you are reaching out and what you are hoping to learn. Respect the other person’s time by keeping your message clear and concise.
  3. Meet and Learn
    Use the conversation to listen, ask thoughtful questions, and understand the person’s experience. Focus on learning rather than asking for opportunities or favors.
  4. Follow Up and Stay Connected
    After the conversation, send a short thank-you message and reflect on what you learned. Staying in touch occasionally helps turn a single interaction into a lasting professional connection.

Outreach Email Templates

Reaching out to someone new can feel intimidating, but a short and thoughtful message is often enough to start a conversation. The templates below can be customized to fit your situation.

Cold Outreach

Use this when you do not already know the person and are reaching out for the first time.

Hello [Name],

My name is [Your Name], and I am a UMSI student interested in learning more about your experience in [role/field]. I came across your profile through [source] and would appreciate the opportunity to ask a few questions if you are open to it.

Thank you for your time,
[Your Name]

Warm Introduction

Use this when you were introduced by a mutual connection.

Hello [Name],

[Mutual contact] suggested that I reach out to you. I am currently a UMSI student exploring [area/role] and would love to learn more about your career path if you have time to connect.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Follow-Up After an Event

Use this after meeting someone at a career event, workshop, or panel.

Hello [Name],

It was great meeting you at [event]. I enjoyed our conversation about [topic] and would appreciate the chance to continue the discussion or ask a few follow-up questions if you are available.

Thank you,
[Your Name]

Informational Interviews Using TIARA

Informational interviews are conversations focused on learning rather than job searching. The TIARA framework helps you ask thoughtful questions while keeping the discussion natural and respectful.

T – Trends
Ask about changes or patterns in the industry or role. Example: What trends are currently shaping your team or field?
I – Insights
Learn what the work is really like on a day-to-day basis. Example: What has surprised you most about this role?
A – Advice
Seek guidance based on their experience. Example: What skills or experiences would you recommend developing?
R – Resources
Ask about tools, materials, or communities that are helpful. Example: Are there resources you recommend for someone entering this field?
A – Assignments
Request small, low-pressure next steps. Example: Is there someone else you suggest I speak with?

Using TIARA can help keep conversations focused on learning and relationship building rather than immediate outcomes.
